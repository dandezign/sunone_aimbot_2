# Deep Research

Deep research is a deliberate, multi-pass investigation that trades speed for
reliability, breadth, and synthesis.

## Philosophy: Evidence-First Sensemaking

Research is a loop: form a hypothesis, find evidence, revise the hypothesis,
and repeat until the signal stabilizes. The goal is not a fast answer; it is a
defensible answer with provenance.

**Before researching, ask**:
- What decision will this inform, and what level of confidence is required?
- What would change the conclusion if new evidence appeared?
- Which sources are authoritative, and which are only directional?

**Core principles**:
1. **Coverage before certainty**: map the landscape, then go deep on winners.
2. **Claims trace to sources**: every key claim must be traceable and scoped.
3. **Iterative refinement**: revise the plan as evidence shifts the question.

## Research Workflow

### 1) Frame and Bound
- Translate the user request into 2-5 research questions.
- Identify constraints (time window, geography, industry scope, source type).
- Define what a good output looks like (table, brief, memo, list of options).

### 2) Plan a Search Strategy
- Start with broad, high-authority sources to anchor definitions.
- Branch into specialized sources for depth and nuance.
- Include at least one counter-source or critical perspective.

### 3) Evidence Collection
- Capture claims, source metadata, dates, and direct quotes when relevant.
- Prefer primary sources (standards, papers, filings) over summaries.
- Track uncertainty and contradictions instead of smoothing them away.

### 4) Synthesis and Reasoning
- Group findings by theme, not by source.
- Weigh evidence quality; separate strong consensus from tentative signals.
- Highlight gaps and recommend what would close them.

### 5) Report with Citations
- Present conclusions first, then support with evidence.
- Use concise citations and include dates for time-sensitive facts.
- State confidence and any material disagreements between sources.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

❌ **Single-source dependence**: Building conclusions on one article.
Why bad: risks bias, errors, and narrow coverage.
Better: triangulate with at least 3 independent sources.

❌ **Source laundering**: Treating secondary summaries as primary evidence.
Why bad: amplifies mistakes and hides provenance.
Better: trace claims back to the original dataset or document.

❌ **Certainty inflation**: Presenting tentative evidence as definitive.
Why bad: misleads decisions and hides uncertainty.
Better: label confidence and show what could change the conclusion.

❌ **Overly broad scope**: Answering everything at once without depth.
Why bad: produces shallow, generic conclusions.
Better: narrow to the highest-impact questions and go deep.

## Variation Guidance

**IMPORTANT**: Outputs must vary with context.
- **Format**: brief memo, comparison table, decision tree, or annotated list.
- **Depth**: quick scan vs. exhaustive review based on stakes.
- **Source mix**: academic, regulatory, industry, or community sources.
- **Voice**: executive summary vs. technical deep dive.

Avoid converging on the same template; match the user's decision context.

## Remember

Deep research is successful when conclusions are traceable, nuanced, and useful
for a real decision. Prioritize rigor over speed.
